## The Core Problem: We Replicated the Format, Not the Mechanics

Our pipeline generates videos that LOOK like viral DM videos. Same chat bubble UI, same GIF breaks, same boy-girl dynamic. But looking like something and functioning like something are different things.

A viral video is an **emotional machine**. Every element serves a specific psychological function. We built a video factory that produces the right shapes but with no emotional engineering inside.

## Phase 0: Taxonomy of Viral Mechanics

Before building anything, we need a shared vocabulary for what actually drives virality in this niche. Based on analyzing all 107 breakdowns, there are **7 mechanical layers** that work together:

### Layer 1: The Scroll-Stop (first 0.5–1.5 seconds)

The viewer is scrolling. Something must make them STOP. In viral DM videos, the scroll-stop is almost always the **opening message itself** — not a title card, not a "How to" label. The message is so outrageous, confusing, or curiosity-inducing that the brain can't keep scrolling.

**What viral hooks do:**
- Create an information gap: "I got 3 things I'm trynna put into you" — *what three things?*
- Trigger a threat response: "can you please spit on me?" — *did he really say that?*
- Break a social norm: "mommy?" — *that's not how you DM a stranger*
- Use familiar setup + unexpected payoff: "do you like water?" — *where is this going?*
- Ask a question that demands resolution: "are you the girl from that movie?" — *what movie?*

**What our hooks do:**
- Describe the video's content: "How to rizz up baddie", "Rookie shooting my shot in ig dms"
- These are YouTube titles, not TikTok hooks. They tell you what you're about to see instead of making you need to see it.

**The rule:** The hook IS the first DM. The title card (if any) should be barely visible. The viewer should feel like they accidentally saw someone's private messages.

### Layer 2: The Reaction Anchor (1.5–4 seconds)

After the hook, the girl's FIRST response determines whether the viewer stays. This response must be a **proxy for the viewer's own reaction**. When the viewer sees "excuse me??" or "HELLO?!?" or "what??", they feel validated — the girl is reacting the way THEY would react. This creates alignment between viewer and content.

**What viral reactions do:**
- Mirror the viewer's shock: "excuse me??", "what??", "hello?!"
- Use emoji as emotion amplifiers: "😭😭", "???"
- Stay extremely short (1–4 words) to let the shock breathe
- NEVER sound like they expected the message

**What our reactions do:**
- Sound like a character playing a role: "Guess I'm your favorite chaos", "bold assumption for a stranger"
- These are witty, but witty isn't the goal. **Authentic visceral shock** is the goal.

### Layer 3: The Tension Engine (4–15 seconds)

This is the back-and-forth that keeps the viewer watching. Viral conversations create tension through **uncertainty** — the viewer genuinely doesn't know if the girl will engage or block him. Each message either escalates the tension or provides a small release before escalating again.

**What viral conversations do:**
- Create genuine will-they-won't-they uncertainty
- Have the girl resist HARD before slowly warming (not through compliments, through cleverness)
- Include multiple "almost walked away" moments
- Use the boy's persistence as entertainment, not his smooth lines
- The boy is often messy, desperate, or chaotic — not suave

**What our conversations do:**
- Follow a predictable arc: pushback → reveal → escalation → ask → number
- The girl's resistance is performative (she always gives in)
- The boy is consistently smooth and confident
- There's no genuine risk of the conversation failing

### Layer 4: The Commentary Layer (memes/clips between messages)

Viral videos use 2–4 clip insertions (avg 2.1s each) that function as a **Greek chorus** — they comment on what just happened and tell the viewer how to feel. These aren't random reaction GIFs.

**What viral clips do:**
- Text overlays that narrate the strategy: "WATCH ME SHOOT MY SHOT", "she took the bait", "gotta be more offensive"
- React to what just happened: "all my aura, gone" after getting rejected
- Build anticipation: "Time to try something else" before a pivot
- Create a parallel sports-metaphor narrative (basketball = shooting your shot)

**What our clips do:**
- Insert random GIFs from a 30-asset library at timing intervals
- No text overlays connecting the clip to the conversation
- No narrative progression across clips

### Layer 5: The Payoff / Twist (final 3–10 seconds)

The ending determines whether the viewer shares, replays, or scrolls past. Viral videos have endings that create a REACTION — not just a resolution.

**What viral endings do:**
- Number exchange with a memorable tease: "text me +1 (digits)" feels like witnessing something real
- Genuine rejection that's funny: the viewer laughs AND empathizes
- Plot twists that reframe everything: "AURA AI" reveal, "my ex's sister btw 💀"
- Cliffhangers that force comments: "pt 2?" "did she reply?"
- The ending must be **screenshottable** — one line someone would send to a friend

**What our endings do:**
- 16 of 17 scripts end with a phone number regardless of arc type
- Even "rejection" arcs give a phone number
- No genuine surprises, twists, or unresolved tension
- The ending is always satisfying — but satisfaction doesn't drive shares. **Reaction** drives shares.

### Layer 6: The Authenticity Signal

Viewers can smell AI-generated content. Real DMs have texture — typos, inconsistent capitalization, emoji bursts, abbreviations, slang. Viral videos lean into this messiness because it signals "this actually happened."

**What viral conversations have:**
- "ain't no way", "you do know im her sister right??", "im jus tryna see"
- "your tryna get both of us in trouble" (intentional misspelling)
- "Seen on Thursday" → "Round 2 🐥" → "Seen 1h ago" → "Round 3 💔" (timestamps showing persistence)
- Emoji as punctuation: "😭😭", "💀", "🐥"
- ALL lowercase, no periods, fragments

**What our conversations have:**
- Proper capitalization: "Guess I'm your favorite chaos"
- Complete sentences with punctuation
- Consistent grammar and tone
- No timestamps, no "seen" indicators, no multi-round persistence

### Layer 7: The Novelty Engine

With 10 videos/day, every video must feel different despite using the same format. Viral creators achieve this not through different words in the same template, but through **different premises that force different conversations**.

**How viral videos stay novel:**
- "mommy?" creates a completely different conversation than "hey I think someone is using your pics to catfish"
- "i just know it's pinker than the dress" forces a specific conversational direction that "do you like water?" never could
- The HOOK determines the CONVERSATION, not the other way around
- Some videos add meta-elements: AI tool reveals, "rounds" of attempts, ex-girlfriend plot twists

**How our videos stay "novel":**
- Same structure, different adjectives
- Arc type labels changed but the underlying conversation didn't
- Every boy is smooth, every girl pushes back then gives in
- Hooks are interchangeable — swapping hooks between scripts wouldn't change the conversation

## Phase 1: Build the Viral Mechanics Scoring Model

**Goal:** Create a quantitative framework that scores any script against the 7 layers above, so we can objectively measure how "viral-aligned" a generated script is before rendering.

### Step 1A: Extract ground-truth scores from viral videos

For each of the 89 viral videos with extracted conversations, compute:

- **Scroll-Stop Score (0–10):** Based on hook shock factor. Score components:
  - Information gap created (0–3)
  - Social norm violation (0–3)
  - Curiosity demand (0–2)
  - Brevity (hooks under 10 words score higher) (0–2)

- **Reaction Anchor Score (0–10):** Based on first girl response:
  - Emotional authenticity (0–3): "excuse me??" = 3, "bold assumption" = 1
  - Brevity (0–2): under 4 words = 2
  - Emoji/punctuation intensity (0–2): "???" or "😭😭" = 2
  - Viewer alignment (0–3): would a real person say exactly this?

- **Tension Score (0–10):** Based on conversation dynamics:
  - Number of genuine resistance moments (0–3)
  - Presence of "almost walked away" beats (0–2)
  - Escalation unpredictability (0–3)
  - Boy's vulnerability/messiness (0–2)

- **Commentary Score (0–10):** Based on clip integration:
  - Clips thematically match preceding message (0–4)
  - Text overlays narrate strategy (0–3)
  - Clips create parallel narrative (0–3)

- **Payoff Score (0–10):** Based on ending:
  - Ending creates a shareable moment (0–4)
  - Ending is surprising or emotionally charged (0–3)
  - Screenshotability of final exchange (0–3)

- **Authenticity Score (0–10):** Based on language texture:
  - Lowercase/no-punctuation ratio (0–2)
  - Slang and abbreviation usage (0–2)
  - Emoji as emotion (not decoration) (0–2)
  - Fragment/incomplete sentence ratio (0–2)
  - Intentional imperfection signals (0–2)

- **Novelty Score (0–10):** Measured as cosine distance from nearest neighbor in the corpus.

### Step 1B: Build the automated scorer

Create `tools/lib/viral-scorer.js` that takes a script JSON and returns scores across all 7 layers. Use:
- NLP heuristics for authenticity (regex patterns for slang, fragments, emoji density)
- Lookup tables for hook shock factor (based on viral hook taxonomy)
- Structural analysis for tension (count resistance beats, check for arc variety)
- Clip-message alignment scoring (semantic similarity between clip context and preceding message)

### Step 1C: Establish baselines

- Score all 89 viral videos → compute mean and standard deviation per layer
- Score all 17 generated scripts → compute same
- The GAP between these distributions is our optimization target
- A script should score within 1 standard deviation of the viral mean on each layer to be considered "viral-aligned"

## Phase 2: Rebuild the Hook System from Scratch

The hook is the single highest-leverage element. A great hook with mediocre banter still gets views. Mediocre hook with great banter gets skipped.

### Step 2A: Classify the 53 viral hooks into hook archetypes

Based on the extracted data, viral hooks fall into distinct psychological categories:

| Archetype | Example | Mechanism | Frequency |
|-----------|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Sexual Shock** | "can you please spit on me?" | Viewer MUST see the reaction | ~25% |
| **Innocent Misdirect** | "do you like water?" | Setup so normal the payoff must be wild | ~15% |
| **Absurd Escalation** | "I would never play hide and seek with you 🥺" | Logic gap forces curiosity | ~15% |
| **Bold Claim** | "I got 3 things I'm trynna put into you" | List/number creates information gap | ~12% |
| **Social Hack** | "hey I think someone is using your pics to catfish" → NASA link | Familiar premise, unexpected pivot | ~10% |
| **Question Trap** | "Why don't you have a boyfriend?" | Forces a revealing answer | ~10% |
| **Meta/Food Gambit** | "Pasta or Steak?", "Burger or Pizza?" | Disarming simplicity → pivot to flirt | ~8% |
| **Compliment Escalation** | "tell your parents I'm a big fan of their work" | Smooth enough to be quotable | ~5% |

### Step 2B: Build a Hook Generator per archetype

Instead of one generic `STORY_REPLY_SYSTEM_PROMPT`, create 8 archetype-specific prompt templates. Each template:
- Defines the psychological mechanism
- Provides 5–8 examples from viral data
- Specifies the EXACT emotional response it should trigger in the girl
- Constrains word count (most viral hooks are 5–15 words)
- Requires the hook to create an information gap that ONLY the conversation can close

### Step 2C: Hook → Conversation coupling

**This is the key insight:** The hook must DETERMINE the conversation, not be independent of it.

Current system: Pick hook → generate conversation → they're basically unrelated.

New system: Pick hook archetype → generate hook → the banter prompt MUST reference the specific hook and build the conversation around resolving the tension/curiosity it created.

Example: If hook is "I got 3 things I'm trynna put into you", the conversation MUST eventually reveal what those 3 things are (and the reveal should be the twist — e.g., "1. respect 2. loyalty 3. this text asking for your number").

## Phase 3: Conversation Architecture Overhaul

### Step 3A: Define 6 conversation structures (not just "arc types")

Our current arc types are labels without structural teeth. We need conversation BLUEPRINTS:

**Structure 1: The Slow Burn (30–45s, 12–18 messages)**
- Hook: Medium shock
- Girl resists for 6+ messages before ANY warmth
- Boy persists with increasing creativity (not desperation)
- Turn happens when boy says something genuinely surprising
- Ends with reluctant number drop + tease

**Structure 2: The Speed Run (15–25s, 6–9 messages)**
- Hook: Maximum shock
- Girl's reaction is so strong it becomes the entertainment
- Rapid-fire exchange with no filler
- Ends before resolution (cliffhanger) or with instant rejection

**Structure 3: The Plot Twist (25–40s, 10–15 messages)**
- Hook: Normal-seeming
- Conversation builds toward expected resolution
- Twist at 70% mark reframes everything (AI reveal, ex-girlfriend, it was a dare, she DM'd him first)
- Ending is the REACTION to the twist, not a phone number

**Structure 4: The Persistence Play (35–50s, 8–12 messages with "seen" gaps)**
- Hook: Gets left on read
- Shows multiple attempts over "time" (Round 1, Round 2, Round 3)
- Includes "Seen" timestamps between rounds
- Each attempt escalates in creativity/desperation
- Boy's persistence itself is the entertainment

**Structure 5: The Reversal (20–35s, 8–12 messages)**
- Hook: Boy starts aggressive/confident
- Girl out-games him — she's funnier, sharper, more provocative
- Boy gets progressively more flustered
- Ends with BOY being the one who's impressed/nervous

**Structure 6: The Compliment Trap (25–35s, 10–14 messages)**
- Hook: Genuine-seeming compliment
- Girl tries to dismiss it
- Boy keeps finding creative ways to reframe the compliment
- Builds to a line so smooth the girl can't help but respond
- The quotable line IS the viral moment

### Step 3B: Rewrite banter prompts per structure

Each structure gets its own system prompt with:
- The emotional journey mapped beat-by-beat
- 3–5 full viral conversation examples as few-shot
- Explicit instructions about WHERE tension builds and releases
- Character voice guidelines specific to this structure (the "persistent" boy sounds different from the "reversal" boy)
- BANNED patterns: no boy being consistently smooth, no girl being consistently dismissive, no predictable resolution timing

### Step 3C: Authenticity injection

Post-processing layer that transforms clean LLM output into "real DM" texture:
- Selective lowercase (80% of messages fully lowercase)
- Remove periods at end of messages (real DMs don't have them)
- Convert "you are" → "youre" or "ur", "I am" → "im", etc.
- Add emoji clusters where emotional intensity peaks ("😭😭", "💀", "???")
- Occasionally split one thought across two messages (the way real texters do)
- Add "Seen" timestamps for Structure 4

## Phase 4: Meme/Clip Layer Redesign

### Step 4A: Build a clip taxonomy

Categorize the 321 viral clip insertions by function:

| Function | Example Overlay | When to Use |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Announcement** | "WATCH ME SHOOT MY SHOT" | Before the hook/opening |
| **Reaction** | "she took the bait" | After girl engages |
| **Strategy** | "gotta be more offensive" | After boy gets rejected |
| **Celebration** | "the play is simply perfect" | After a smooth line lands |
| **Desperation** | "all my aura, gone" | After a hard rejection |
| **Pivot** | "Time to try something else" | Before boy changes approach |

### Step 4B: Clip selection by conversation context

Instead of random clip assignment, create a mapping:
- After hook delivery → "Announcement" clip
- After girl's shock reaction → "Reaction" clip
- After girl resists → "Strategy" clip
- After boy's best line → "Celebration" clip
- After rejection → "Desperation" clip

### Step 4C: Text overlay generation

The text overlays on clips are part of the narrative. Generate them contextually:
- Keep them short (3–7 words)
- They should comment on the action like a friend watching over your shoulder
- They should use sports/competition metaphors ("shooting my shot", "game recognize game")
- They create anticipation for what comes next

## Phase 5: Duration & Pacing Restructure

### Step 5A: Extend video duration

Our 15–21s videos are critically short. Viral videos average 50–60s. This isn't a cosmetic difference — longer videos get more watch time, which the algorithm rewards.

Target durations by structure:
- Speed Run: 18–25s
- Slow Burn: 35–45s
- Plot Twist: 30–40s
- Persistence Play: 40–55s
- Reversal: 25–35s
- Compliment Trap: 28–38s

### Step 5B: Pacing rhythm

Viral video pacing follows a specific pattern:
- **Messages:** 3–5 seconds of chat (2–3 messages visible)
- **Clip break:** 1.5–2.5 seconds of reaction meme
- **Messages:** 3–5 seconds of chat
- **Clip break:** 1.5–2.5 seconds
- Repeat, with the gaps getting SHORTER as tension builds
- Final 3–4 messages come rapid-fire with no clip breaks

This creates an accelerating rhythm that builds urgency toward the ending.

## Phase 6: The Novelty Engine (10/day without repetition)

### Step 6A: Hook rotation system

With 8 hook archetypes and 10 videos/day:
- Daily distribution: 3 Sexual Shock, 2 Innocent Misdirect, 1 Absurd Escalation, 1 Bold Claim, 1 Social Hack, 1 Question Trap, 1 rotating
- Never repeat the same archetype twice consecutively
- Track all generated hooks in a history file; reject any with >60% word overlap to a previous hook

### Step 6B: Structure rotation

- Daily distribution: 2 Slow Burn, 2 Speed Run, 2 Plot Twist, 1 Persistence Play, 2 Reversal, 1 Compliment Trap
- Pair hooks with structures intentionally (Sexual Shock → Speed Run, Social Hack → Plot Twist, etc.)

### Step 6C: Variable combinations

Novelty dimensions to rotate across:
- Hook archetype (8 options)
- Conversation structure (6 options)
- Spice tier (3 options)
- Girl personality (expand from 5 to 10+ distinct voices)
- Boy personality (expand from 5 to 10+ distinct voices)
- Ending type (phone number, rejection, cliffhanger, twist, escalation)
- Meme theme (sports, reactions, celebrations, cringe)

8 × 6 × 3 × 10 × 10 × 5 × 4 = **288,000 unique combinations** before any hook text or dialogue is even considered.

## Phase 7: Feedback Loop & Weekly Optimization

### Step 7A: Post-and-measure workflow

1. Generate 10 scripts/day
2. Score all 10 with the viral mechanics scorer
3. Render top 3–4 (highest combined score)
4. Post 2–3 per day
5. After 48 hours, record view counts for each posted video
6. Map view counts back to their viral mechanics scores

### Step 7B: Weekly pattern analysis

Every 7 days:
- Which hook archetype got the most views?
- Which conversation structure performed best?
- Which specific lines got the most engagement (comments, shares)?
- Reweight the daily distribution based on performance data
- Identify any new patterns from comment analysis

### Step 7C: A/B testing framework

Each week, run one controlled experiment:
- Week 1: Test Sexual Shock hooks vs. Innocent Misdirect hooks (same structure, same clip style)
- Week 2: Test 20s videos vs. 40s videos (same hook type)
- Week 3: Test with text overlay clips vs. without
- Week 4: Test "phone number ending" vs. "cliffhanger ending"

One variable at a time. Let the data tell us what the audience actually responds to.

## Implementation Priority

If I had to rank what would move the needle MOST with the least code change:

1. **Fix the hooks** (Phase 2) — this alone could 5–10x views. The hook determines whether anyone sees the rest. No amount of great banter matters if nobody stops scrolling.

2. **Extend duration + fix pacing** (Phase 5) — going from 17s to 35–45s doubles watch time, which the algorithm directly rewards.

3. **Authenticity injection** (Phase 3C) — making the text feel like real DMs instead of LLM output. Costs almost nothing to implement (post-processing layer) but dramatically changes perception.

4. **Conversation structures** (Phase 3A-B) — moving from 1 formula to 6 distinct blueprints creates the variety needed for 10/day.

5. **Clip redesign** (Phase 4) — contextual meme selection instead of random. Makes videos feel curated instead of assembled.

6. **Scoring model** (Phase 1) — the analytical backbone that makes everything measurable. Without this, we're guessing.

7. **Feedback loop** (Phase 7) — the engine that compounds learning over time. Not urgent for day 1 but essential for week 4+.

## The North Star

The goal isn't to copy viral videos. It's to understand the **emotional engineering** behind them deeply enough that we can generate infinite novel videos that trigger the same psychological responses.

Every viral video in our dataset is a different story told through the same emotional machine. We need to build that machine — not copy the stories.
